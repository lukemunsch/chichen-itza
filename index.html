Mayan Ruins of Mexico

CHEE-CHIN EET-SA

The pre-Columbian ruins built by the Maya people of the Terminal Classic period.

These ruins are highly popular with tourists from all around the world; they are steeped in classic and recogniseable building styles and imagery, they are the source of many daytrips along with the 4 cenote (naturally formed sinkholes), and has been recognised as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Yucatán, Mexico

These ruins are built in the Eastern section of Yucatán state of Mexico. The size of the site spans across 8 square kilometres, much of which has been excavated. There are twelve main highlights at the ruins, including the Temple of the Feathered Serpent Kukulkan (El Castillo), the hall of the thousand columns, the temple of the warriors and the great Ballcourt. Settlers would have first arrived around 250-500ad and would have been home to nearly 35 000 people

Travel

Cancún and the Mayan Riviera are popular sites for travel in Mexico, being beach locales and their vast cultural attractions. They are also close to the main sites to visit; Chichén-Itzá and its smaller but also popular Temple of Tulum.

Chichén-Itzá is located about 120 miles west of Cancún; while most people travel for the beach experience and the ruins, there are numerous nightlife venues and city break style resorts for people to visit as well.


Tourism

The site has been one of Mexico’s most visited archeological landmarks and many people on holiday who stay in Cancún and other numerous hotels in the area.

These ruins have named a World heritage site since 1998. During its nearly 1 millennium history, numerous people have left their mark on the city; artistic monuments and designs of the world and universe have been created by Maya and Toltec people.

Chichén-Itzá’s Temple of Kukulkan was voted a New wonder of the world since 2017 by a poll of 100million people by the “New Open World Corporation”. This Pyramid has been the instant source of archeological visits and is still turning out new discoveries to this day.

Popularity of the site has been increasing over the last decade with over 2.1 million people visitors a year since 2014 - reaching 2.7 million in 2018. The most popular time of the year is the Spring Equinox (around March 30th); during this time, the midday sun creates a shadow of ’The Feathered Seprent’ the crawls up the central pyramid Temple of Kukulkan.

Many people take day tours of Chichén-Itzá with trips around other local sites; 4 naturally formed sinkholes. The Cenote Sagrado, or ’Sacred Cenote’ is 60ft wide with 29ft sheer cliff faces down to the water and it has been investigated thoroughly; researchers have found offers and sacrifices of carved jade, copal, obsidian, pottery, shell, wood, cloth, gold, as well as skeletons of children and men. The couple of the other cenote popular with the tour guides can even be swam in.
